# Using GitHub Copilot in Non-CS Courses

GitHub Copilot isn't just for computer science. As an AI-powered assistant available at [github.com/copilot](https://github.com/copilot), Copilot can help students and educators across every discipline — from writing and research to data analysis and creative projects. Verified teachers through GitHub Education have access to Copilot Pro, which includes unlimited chats and additional features.

This guide provides discipline-specific ideas and resources for bringing Copilot into courses outside of traditional programming.

**Learning Objectives:**
- Understand how GitHub Copilot can support teaching and learning beyond software development
- Explore practical use cases for Copilot across the humanities, sciences, social sciences and creative arts
- Learn prompting strategies that help students think critically while using AI
- Develop classroom guidelines for responsible and effective AI use

**Prerequisites:** 
- A GitHub account with Copilot enabled ([setup guide](https://docs.github.com/en/copilot/setting-up-github-copilot/setting-up-github-copilot-for-yourself))
- Familiarity with the [Getting Started with GitHub Copilot](https://github.com/github-education-resources/teacher-toolbox/blob/main/Learning-Pathways/Getting-started-with-copilot.md) learning pathway is recommended but not required

---

## How Copilot Works Outside of Code

While Copilot was built as a coding assistant, its chat interface ([github.com/copilot](https://github.com/copilot)) is a general-purpose AI that can:

- **Brainstorm and outline** — generate essay structures, research questions, or project plans
- **Explain and summarize** — break down complex topics, papers, or datasets in plain language
- **Analyze and transform data** — help write formulas, clean data, or interpret results (even in spreadsheets)
- **Draft and revise** — support the writing process as a feedback partner, not a replacement for student voice
- **Translate and localize** — assist multilingual classrooms and international research

> 💡 **Key principle:** Position Copilot as a *thinking partner*, not an answer machine. The best classroom use cases are ones where students must evaluate, refine, and build upon Copilot's output.

## Discipline-Specific Use Cases

### 📝 Writing and Composition
- **Outlining and brainstorming:** Students prompt Copilot with a thesis statement and ask for counterarguments, supporting evidence structures, or alternative angles
- **Peer review simulation:** Before a human peer review, students ask Copilot to critique their draft and then write a reflection on which AI suggestions they accepted or rejected and why
- **Citation and source discovery:** Ask Copilot to suggest search terms, databases, or related authors for a research topic (always verify — AI can hallucinate sources)

**Sample prompt for students:**
> "I'm writing an argumentative essay about urban green spaces and public health. Give me three counterarguments to my position that cities should mandate rooftop gardens, and suggest what kind of evidence I'd need to refute each one."

### 🔬 Sciences (Biology, Chemistry, Physics, Environmental Science)
- **Lab report assistance:** Students describe their experiment and ask Copilot to help structure a methods section or identify gaps in their procedure
- **Data interpretation:** Paste in a small dataset and ask Copilot to suggest what statistical test to use and why
- **Concept exploration:** Ask Copilot to explain a concept at different levels (e.g., "Explain CRISPR as if I'm a high school student, then as if I'm a graduate student")

**Sample prompt for students:**
> "I measured plant growth under three light conditions over 14 days. Here's my data: [paste data]. What type of chart should I use to present this, and what statistical test would tell me if the differences are significant?"

### 📊 Social Sciences (Psychology, Sociology, Economics, Political Science)
- **Survey and interview design:** Ask Copilot to help refine survey questions to reduce bias or suggest follow-up probes for qualitative interviews
- **Policy analysis:** Give Copilot a policy brief and ask it to identify stakeholders, potential unintended consequences, or comparable policies from other regions
- **Literature review scaffolding:** Describe your research question and ask Copilot to suggest categories for organizing your literature review

**Sample prompt for students:**
> "I'm studying the impact of remote work on employee wellbeing. Help me organize a literature review outline with 4-5 thematic categories, and suggest what kind of studies I should look for in each category."

### 🎨 Creative Arts and Media
- **Storyboarding and narrative design:** Use Copilot to brainstorm plot structures, character arcs, or dialogue variations
- **Design critique:** Describe a visual design or composition and ask Copilot for feedback based on design principles
- **Music and composition:** Ask Copilot to explain music theory concepts, suggest chord progressions, or help with lyric writing as a creative exercise

**Sample prompt for students:**
> "I'm designing a poster for a campus sustainability campaign. My current concept uses a wilting flower transforming into a solar panel. Give me feedback on this visual metaphor and suggest two alternative concepts that might communicate urgency more effectively."

### 🌍 Languages and Humanities (History, Philosophy, Foreign Languages)
- **Translation and language practice:** Students write in a target language, then ask Copilot to identify grammar errors and explain the rules behind each correction
- **Primary source analysis:** Paste a historical document excerpt and ask Copilot to identify rhetorical strategies, historical context, or potential biases
- **Philosophical dialogue:** Ask Copilot to argue a position from a specific philosophical framework (e.g., "How would a utilitarian respond to this ethical dilemma?")

**Sample prompt for students:**
> "Here is a paragraph I wrote in Spanish about climate change. Identify any grammar or vocabulary errors, explain why each is incorrect, and suggest a more natural phrasing. Don't rewrite the whole paragraph — I want to fix it myself."

### 📐 Mathematics and Statistics
- **Problem-solving guidance:** Students describe a problem they're stuck on and ask Copilot for hints (not solutions) about which approach to try
- **Formula explanation:** Ask Copilot to explain a formula in plain language, with a real-world analogy
- **Data analysis with spreadsheets:** Ask Copilot to help write Excel or Google Sheets formulas, pivot table strategies, or simple Python/R scripts for data coursework

**Sample prompt for students:**
> "I'm trying to understand the Central Limit Theorem. Explain it to me using an everyday analogy, then walk me through how I would demonstrate it with dice rolls."

## Classroom Guidelines for Responsible Use

Every course is different, but here are principles you can adapt for your syllabus:

1. **Transparency:** Require students to disclose when and how they used Copilot (e.g., an "AI use statement" appended to assignments)
2. **Critical evaluation:** Make evaluating AI output part of the assignment — "What did Copilot get wrong? What would you change?"
3. **Process over product:** Grade the thinking process, not just the final output. Reflection logs, revision histories, and prompt journals help
4. **Academic integrity:** Define clear boundaries — e.g., "You may use Copilot for brainstorming and outlining but not for generating final draft text"
5. **Equity:** Remind students that Copilot is free with a GitHub account, so all students have access

> For a comprehensive framework, see [TeachAI's AI Guidance for Schools Toolkit](https://www.teachai.org/toolkit) and Microsoft's [Responsible use of AI in education](https://learn.microsoft.com/en-us/training/paths/responsible-use-of-artificial-intelligence-in-education/?source=recommendations) learning path.
